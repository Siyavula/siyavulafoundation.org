The value and impact of building skills and knowledge in Mathematics and Physical Sciences extend far beyond the classroom. They are subjects that encourage critical and analytical thinking, as well as creativity and exploration. They help develop problem-solving skills, and teach learners to work collaboratively. These skills are needed desperately in the world of tomorrow, where innovation, growth and change will happen at a faster and faster rate. Our mission is to provide all students with the tools and resources they will need to excel in Mathematics, Physics & Chemistry at school, and in addition provide a springboard for future opportunities and study. Our aim is to reduce the inequality in our education system in three ways: By giving learners free access to a world-class online practice platform that allows them to master the skills they need to excel in Mathematics, Physics & Chemistry. By providing access to curriculum-aligned textbooks on our website, which are free for learners to read, download and even print. By using our large-scale monitoring system to provide unique insights and research to national and provincial education departments, as well as to individual schools and teachers. Although our roots are in South Africa, our vision is to extend our reach across the African continent, to provide students with free access to world-class education resources in maths and science. Research insights for data-driven decisions Education can benefit from making data-driven decisions. Our data, which captures every interaction a learner has on our system allows us to visualise and interpret the data we collect for a whole school, province or country. This large-scale monitoring system provides unique insights that can help answer questions like: How and at what rate are learners progressing through the curriculum? What is the current level of mastery of learners in a school, district, province or the country? When and how are learners practising throughout the day? What does this tell us about access to technology in these contexts? What is the aggregated level of effort for learners in a particular school, district or province? What are the most common problem areas or misconceptions, and do these vary in different contexts? Can we identify classes, schools or districts where intervention is most needed, based on current performance?
